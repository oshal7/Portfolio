00 / overview

Knowing you can recover, before you have to

Cohesity protects some of the world's most critical enterprise data. But protection has historically been passive — policies run, snapshots accumulate, and the first real test of the system happens on the worst possible day: during an actual incident.

Recovery Readiness reframes the product experience around active resilience — continuously answering the question every administrator quietly worries about: "If something goes wrong right now, how fast and how completely can we recover?"

01 / context

Backups were a checkbox. Recovery was a leap of faith.

In the existing model, success was measured by backup completion: green checkmarks, met SLAs, storage consumed. Teams could go months — sometimes years — without ever exercising a real recovery.

Meanwhile the stakes kept rising. Ransomware turned recovery from an occasional IT event into a board-level concern. Compliance frameworks began asking not "do you have backups?" but "when did you last prove you could restore?" The industry was moving from data protection to data resilience — and the product experience needed to move with it.

02 / the problem

Green dashboards were hiding real risk

Research sessions with administrators surfaced an uncomfortable pattern: teams with fully "healthy" backup dashboards still failed recovery drills. Stale credentials, changed network topologies, missing dependencies, and untested runbooks meant that backup success was a poor proxy for recovery success.

🟢

False confidence

Administrators equated backup completion with recoverability. The dashboard rewarded the wrong behavior — collecting snapshots instead of proving restores.

🧪

Recovery was never rehearsed

Full recovery drills were rare, manual, and disruptive — so most teams simply didn't run them. The first end-to-end restore was often during a live incident.

⏱️

Unknown recovery time

Nobody could confidently state their actual RTO. Estimates existed on paper; reality diverged the moment infrastructure changed.

How might we make recovery readiness continuously visible and testable — so that resilience becomes a daily practice instead of a yearly fire drill?

cohesity.com / resilience / readiness

03 / design approach

Design the question, not just the dashboard

The core design move was shifting the primary object of the experience from backups (what the system does) to readiness (what the user actually needs to know). Everything else followed from that reframe.

01

Define what "ready" means

Worked with recovery engineers and customers to break readiness into measurable dimensions — backup integrity, environment drift, dependency mapping, and drill recency.

02

Make readiness a score, not a log

Distilled those dimensions into a single, explainable readiness signal per workload — with clear drill-downs into exactly what's dragging it and how to fix it.

03

Rehearsal without disruption

Designed non-disruptive recovery drills — isolated, scheduled test recoveries that validate the full path without touching production.

04

Turn incidents into muscle memory

Drill results feed runbooks and readiness history, so every rehearsal makes the real recovery faster and calmer.

04 / final solution

Resilience you can see, schedule, and prove

The final experience centers on a readiness score for every critical workload — continuously computed, always explainable. Administrators see at a glance which workloads are provably recoverable, which are drifting, and exactly what to do about it.

A drill scheduler makes rehearsal routine: pick a workload, pick a window, and the platform runs an isolated test recovery — then reports actual recovery time against the promised RTO. Readiness history turns every drill into evidence for audits, leadership, and the team's own confidence.

05 / outcome & impact

From "we think so" to "we know so"

Early validation with enterprise customers showed the reframe landing exactly where it needed to — in how teams talk about and practice recovery.

Score
readiness became the first thing admins check — ahead of backup status
Drills
non-disruptive rehearsals moved recovery testing from yearly to routine
RTO
teams report actual, drill-verified recovery times instead of estimates

Beyond the metrics, the biggest shift is cultural: recovery stopped being a hypothetical. Teams using readiness scores and drills walk into incidents with a rehearsed plan — resilience as a practice, not a promise.
